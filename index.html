The Big Question

Can Recall Data Provide Market Insight for a Strong Investment Strategy?

Every vehicle recall leaves a trail of breadcrumbs weeks before the public announcement — in consumer complaints, government investigations, and technical service bulletins. The market ignores these signals. We built a system to capture them.

01
Noise Window — Our Edge

Pre-Event Signals

Complaint spikes appear in the NHTSA database. TSBs accelerate. Investigations open. The market ignores all of it. Our XGBoost model quantifies these signals and fires a STRONG SIGNAL 30–90 days before the recall is public. Riverdale enters the short position here.

02
Drift Window — Position Grows

Institutional Awareness Builds

NHTSA opens a formal investigation — now a public signal. Financial news picks it up. Analysts flag it. Institutional money begins positioning. The stock drifts down gradually as awareness builds. Riverdale's position is already profitable and growing.

03
Filing Date — Exit at T+1

Market Catches Up

Recall officially filed. Our event study confirms mean CAR ≈ 0% at filing — the market had already priced it in during Phase 2. Riverdale exits the position at T+1 (day after filing) having captured the full drift. This resolves the apparent contradiction between Semi-Strong EMH and our informational edge.

"The market generally fails to impound the information contained in customer complaints in a timely manner — even among sophisticated institutional investors like mutual funds."

Stage 1 — Signal Model

XGBoost Recall Probability Model

A supervised machine learning model trained on 121,137 monthly observations from 2000–2025. It predicts whether a specific make/model will have a recall filed within 90 days — firing 30–90 days before any public announcement.

Why XGBoost? — Supervised Machine Learning

XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting) is a supervised machine learning method — meaning it learns from labeled historical data where the outcome (recall or no recall) is known. It builds hundreds of sequential decision trees, each one learning from the previous tree's mistakes. Unlike standard Gradient Boosting, XGBoost adds built-in L1 and L2 regularization to prevent overfitting, handles missing data natively, and runs significantly faster. Think of it as a team of analysts where each new analyst focuses specifically on the cases the previous one got wrong — the final prediction combines all of their opinions.

XGBoost was selected because it matches the methodology of our peer-reviewed source — Li et al. (2025), a Q1-ranked Scimago paper published in Risk Analysis, which applied XGBoost to NHTSA consumer complaints to predict massive vehicle recalls. The AUC gap between XGBoost and Gradient Boosting in our run was only 0.0072 — statistically negligible — confirming both perform equivalently while XGBoost provides additional literature validation.

60 Features Across 5 Data Sources

velocity_spike_ratio

Importance: 0.1823 — #1 Feature

30-day complaints divided by 12-month baseline per make/model. A 5x spike means complaints are arriving 5× faster than normal. Most predictive single feature at 18.2% of model decisions.

complaint_count (30d / 3m / 6m)

Importance: 0.1204 / 0.0743 / 0.0487

Raw complaint volume across time windows. Total NHTSA consumer complaints for this specific make/model in the last 30, 90, and 180 days.

comp_spike_ratio

Importance: 0.0891 — #3 Feature

Component-specific spike ratio. Same velocity logic applied to the dominant complaint component (e.g., airbags, brakes). Isolates targeted surges from general complaint volume.

inv_count_180d

Importance: 0.0612 — #5 Feature

Open NHTSA investigations for this manufacturer in the 180-day window. Multiple simultaneous investigations signal elevated regulatory scrutiny.

crash_count / injury_count / death_count

Importance: 0.0534 / 0.0163 / 0.0061

Complaints that also report a crash, fire, or injury. Severity indicators — a spike in injury complaints is a much stronger recall predictor than general volume alone.

tsb_count_180d / tsb_velocity_30d

Importance: 0.0423 / 0.0218

Technical Service Bulletins filed in 180 days and TSB acceleration. TSBs are manufacturer notices to dealers quietly acknowledging known problems — a surge often precedes a recall.

airbag / brake / steering complaints

Importance: 0.0241 / 0.0196 / 0.0097

High-risk component complaints. Complaints specifically mentioning the most dangerous systems — these are stronger recall predictors than general complaint counts.

R2 — Structural Break Dummy

Importance: 0.0171 — Rank #16

= 1 for observations 2014M03–2021M01 (Takata era). Identified by Bai-Perron structural break test. Recall rate was 16.1% vs 7.4% baseline — the model adjusts for this structural shift.

R3 — Structural Break Dummy

Importance: 0.0124 — Rank #20

= 1 for observations from 2021M02 onward (EV era). Tesla, Rivian, Lucid enter the recall universe. Software recalls increase. The model adjusts recall probability expectations for this modern era.

"Paye and Timmermann (2006) document that financial prediction models exhibit instability across structural break periods. By including structural break dummies and testing exclusively on Regime 3 (which the model never trained on), we verify the model maintains AUC 0.8083 on a regime it had never seen — confirming it captured genuine signal rather than era-specific noise."

— Supported by: Paye & Timmermann (2006), Journal of Empirical Finance; Khanna et al. (2025), arXiv:2511.09754

Temporal Train / Test Split — No Lookahead Bias

  • Train: 2000M01 – 2020M12 (97,555 rows) — entirely Regime 1 and Regime 2
  • Test: 2021M01 – 2025M09 (23,582 rows) — entirely Regime 3
  • 90-day buffer: Oct–Dec 2025 removed to prevent right-censoring. Complaint spikes in this window cannot confirm whether a recall followed within 90 days — including them would create false negatives.
  • 2026 data: Held out entirely for profit estimation — genuinely unseen

Why Testing on Regime 3 Is Especially Valuable

The model trained only on R1 and R2 — it never saw a single post-2021 EV-era observation. Testing on R3 is the hardest possible test: a completely different manufacturer landscape (Tesla, Rivian, Lucid), software recalls, and post-COVID supply chain dynamics. AUC of 0.8083 on this unseen regime directly contradicts the finding in Khanna et al. (2025) that "most models exhibit sharp performance declines when tested on unseen regimes." This gives Riverdale confidence the edge is durable across future regimes.

Stage 2 — Position Sizing

Kelly Criterion — Optimal Capital Allocation

A mathematical formula developed by John Kelly (1956) and applied to financial markets by Edward Thorp. It determines the optimal fraction of your portfolio to allocate to each trade given a known win rate and payoff.

Full Kelly Formula
f* = (p × b − q) / b
Where: f* = optimal fraction | p = win probability | b = win size / stop loss | q = 1 − p
In Simple Terms
Position size = (how often it works × how much you make) minus (how often it fails × your stop loss)
Position Scaling Formula
Final Position = Base × (Signal Probability / Threshold)
Capped at $1,000,000 (10% of AUM) | Floored at $50,000 (0.5% of AUM)
p — Win Rate
From Recall Impact Scorecard — how often this company's stock drops more than 2% on recall days
Base rate: 21.9% across all 3,631 recall events
b — Win Size
Average drop size from Recall Impact Scorecard divided by stop loss. Bigger drops = better risk/reward
Example — Lucid: -11.66% avg drop → positive Kelly → $604,000 base position
q — Loss Rate
1 − p. How often the stock does NOT drop and the short position loses to the stop loss
Stop loss: fixed at 5% — maximum loss per trade before exit
Half-Kelly Applied
We use half the Kelly fraction (f*/2) to account for estimation error in the win rate and reduce volatility
Standard practice in institutional Kelly Criterion applications

Portfolio Parameters

  • Portfolio size: $10,000,000 — consistent with emerging manager AUM per The Hedge Fund Journal (2023), which defines emerging managers as funds under $100M
  • Max position: $1,000,000 (10% concentration cap)
  • Min floor: $50,000 (0.5%) — for negative-Kelly companies where drops are statistically real but unconditional Kelly is negative
  • Market impact: At $1M max position, even the least liquid holding (Lucid at ~$80M daily volume) represents <2% of ADV — zero market impact per Almgren & Chriss (2000)

Top 10 Signals Per Month — Why?

The Kelly Criterion already scales position size by signal probability — higher confidence signals receive larger positions. Beyond the top 10, signal probabilities drop significantly, meaning Kelly would assign near-floor positions anyway.

Selecting the top 10 per month ensures every deployed dollar is behind a signal where the model has genuine conviction. It is also consistent with real-world systematic fund management practice — a $10M fund does not simultaneously hold 120 short positions across one sector.

"The Kelly Criterion provides the optimal bet size for maximizing the long-run growth rate of capital, balancing risk and reward mathematically rather than arbitrarily."

— Hung (2010), Betting with the Kelly Criterion; Thorp (1997), The Kelly Criterion in Blackjack, Sports Betting, and the Stock Market
The Complete System

Three-Stage Investment Pipeline

From raw NHTSA data to a sized short position — the complete signal-to-trade flow.

01
Stage 1 — Recall Probability Model
XGBoost scans NHTSA complaints, investigations, TSBs, and structural break dummies monthly for all 17 manufacturers. Assigns recall probability (0–100%) to each make/model/month observation. Fires STRONG SIGNAL above the 17.15% threshold — validated by three statistical tests at p = 0.000000.
OUTPUT → STRONG SIGNAL with probability score
02
Stage 2 — Recall Impact Scorecard
Maps each STRONG SIGNAL to the company's historical stock behavior on recall days. Built from 3,631 recall events (2000–2025). Provides drop rate (% of recalls where CAR < -2%), average drop size, and base position via Kelly Criterion. Ferrari is the only company excluded — t-test p = 1.0, drops are statistical noise.
OUTPUT → Base position ($50,000 floor to $604,000 Lucid)
03
Stage 3 — Execution
Final position = Base × (Signal Probability / Threshold), capped at $1M. Short position entered at start of observation month. Hold for 45 days (survival analysis median: Kaplan-Meier + Cox PH model determined 44-day median signal-to-recall time) or exit at T+1 if recall files earlier. 5% stop loss enforced throughout.
OUTPUT → Short trade entered, monitored, exited

45-Day Hold — Determined by Survival Analysis

The hold period is not arbitrary. A Kaplan-Meier survival analysis combined with a Cox Proportional Hazards model was run on historical signal-to-recall-filing times. The median time from a STRONG SIGNAL to actual recall filing was 44 days, rounded to 45. This means half of all predicted recalls file within the hold window — the position is sized and timed to capture the median expected move. Key survival analysis predictors: inv_count_180d (p < 0.0001) and high_risk_count (p = 0.0015).

Publicly Traded Companies Only — Why

Short selling requires a publicly traded security with a liquid market, available price data, and shares that can be borrowed. Private companies or private subsidiaries have no mechanism for the market to react through price movement. This is not a choice — it is a structural requirement of the short selling mechanism. Mitsubishi was dropped due to insufficient recall data for statistical analysis. Ferrari passes the data threshold but fails the t-test (p = 1.0). The remaining 16 companies form the tradeable universe.

Why -2% CAR as the Drop Threshold?

A stock drop of -2% or more on a recall day is considered a meaningful market reaction — not random daily noise. This threshold is consistent with event study methodology and supported by statistical significance testing. A company's recall-day drops are included in the drop rate calculation only when CAR < -2%, ensuring we measure genuine negative market reactions rather than normal trading volatility.

Why Only Lucid Has a Positive Kelly Fraction

Lucid (38.5% drop rate, -11.66% average drop) is the only company where the unconditional Kelly fraction is positive (+0.1208), producing a $604,000 base position. All other companies have negative unconditional Kelly — meaning at their raw historical drop rates, the expected value is negative at base sizing. However, these companies become viable when Stage 1's STRONG SIGNAL fires: the 4.4× informational edge pushes the effective win rate well above the raw historical rate, making the $50,000 floor position viable in expectation. The floor is not arbitrary — it represents the minimum deployment where Stage 1's edge justifies the trade.
